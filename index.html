What's Dia de Muertos?

It's considered perhaps the most representative tradition of Mexican culture. The celebration is made on two days: Novemeber 1st, dedicated to deceased children and November 2nd to celebrate the adults. The origin of the tradition can be traced back to the ancient civilizations of Mexico, but it also incorporates Spanish features due to the intermingling and interchanging of both races.

Locations

Even though the festivity is celebrated all across Mexico, we've selected the top locations for you to have a full Dia de Muertos experience

San Andrés Mixquic

The town of San Andrés Mixquic is one of the most visited during Dia de Muertos. The events of the day culminate in the Alumbrada, when the cemetery that surrounds the community’s main church glows with thousands of candles and smoke from incense.

Pátzcuaro & Janitzio

Located in the state of Michoacán, the enigmantic island of Janitzio is illuminated with candles from the hundreds of processions the transport the offerings for the dead. Don't miss a chance to also taste the local cuisine!

Xico

Designated as one of Mexico's 'Magic Towns' this is one of the best site to witness the splendour of Dia de Muertos. The celebration starts since October 28th and ends on the evening of November 2nd as thousands gather in the cementery to spend the night. An impressive carpet of flowers covers the place.

Xochimilco

Spending Dia de Muertos at Xochimilco is something you dont want to miss! This neighbourhood is popular due to its prehispanic canals and 'trajineras' (boats) that make night tours to a small island, La Isla de las Muñecas where the famous legend of La Llorona is represented. Don't miss a chance to eat the traditional pan de muerto!
